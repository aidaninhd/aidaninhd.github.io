Historical Timeline

Late 1800s – Early Concepts

1897: Karl Ferdinand Braun invents the first cathode ray tube (CRT), which is essentially an oscilloscope used for displaying electrical waveforms.
1907: Boris Rosing integrates a CRT with a mechanical scanner to create a rudimentary television display system.

1920s – CRT Becomes a Display Technology

1925: John Logie Baird pioneers the use of CRT for displaying television images in his experimental systems.
1929: Vladimir Zworykin develops the kinescope, a CRT-based receiver used for television systems, laying the foundation for future development.

1930s – Commercial Breakthrough

1934: Telefunken and Philco release the first commercial CRT televisions in Germany and the U.S., though still very expensive and low in picture quality.
1939: RCA introduces its first CRT television at the New York World’s Fair, marking one of the first large public demonstrations of television.

1940s – Post-War Expansion

1946: After World War II, mass production of televisions begins, and CRT televisions start to enter consumer homes in significant numbers.
1948: Color TV experiments with CRT technology begin in the U.S., but widespread adoption is still years away.

1950s – First Color CRTs

1954: RCA introduces the CT-100, the first mass-produced color CRT television, marking the official launch of consumer color TV.
1956: The FCC approves color broadcasting standards, pushing CRT technology into a new era.

1960s – CRT Becomes a Household Staple

1965: Over 50 million CRT TVs are sold in the U.S., making it a common household item.
1968: Sony launches the Trinitron, revolutionizing CRT design with its aperture grille system, providing superior color and sharpness compared to traditional shadow-mask CRTs.

1970s – Widespread Color TV Adoption

1972: Color CRT TVs outsell black-and-white models for the first time in the U.S.
1975: Sony's Trinitron becomes the global standard for CRT televisions, winning an Emmy Award for engineering excellence.

1980s – Enhanced Picture Quality

1983: Sony releases the first flat-screen CRT, further improving picture quality and reducing glare.
1987: High-resolution CRT monitors start gaining popularity in computer displays, marking a shift toward higher-definition capabilities.

1990s – Rise of Larger and Flat-Screen CRTs

1990: Superfine Pitch CRTs by Sony deliver even higher resolution and sharper images, a response to the increasing demand for better picture quality.
1997: Widescreen CRTs (16:9 aspect ratio) enter the market, anticipating the digital TV revolution.

2000s – Decline and Obsolescence

2001: Flat-panel displays (LCD and plasma) begin to outsell CRTs due to their slimmer profile and reduced weight.
2004: CRT sales rapidly decline as consumers switch to flat-panel displays.
2008: Sony discontinues the production of its legendary Trinitron, symbolizing the end of the CRT era.

2010s – End of Consumer CRTs

2010: CRT TVs and monitors largely disappear from stores as flat-panel displays dominate.
2013: The last major TV manufacturers cease production of CRT televisions.

2020s – CRTs in Retro Gaming and Collecting

2020: CRTs see a resurgence in demand among retro gaming enthusiasts, who appreciate the display’s compatibility and aesthetic with older consoles.
